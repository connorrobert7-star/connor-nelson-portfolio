There is a specific quality of October light in northern Michigan that does not photograph. You can try — and I have tried, for years — but the camera doesn't know what it's looking at. The light isn't golden in the way autumn light is supposed to be golden. It's something more surgical. More specific. It comes in at an angle that suggests it knows exactly where it's going, and it has been calibrated for this landscape and no other.

The trees help. The hardwoods in northern Michigan don't fade gracefully from green to color. They turn in patches. A sugar maple will be three different shades on three different branches, and for a week or so in early October, before the hard frosts lock everything in, the effect is of something burning quietly and not consumed.

---

I have driven Twenty Mile Road in every season. Winter is the most honest: the road is just a road, and the fields beside it are just fields, and the sky does exactly what Michigan skies do in winter, which is to become the same color as the ground and leave you navigating by texture alone.

But October is different. October is the month when the road becomes aware of you.

I don't know how else to put it. There are places — I think everyone who lives in rural places knows this — where you feel, under certain conditions, that the landscape registers your presence. Not hostilely. Not welcomingly. Just: it notes you. You are added to some account. A tallying that has been going on longer than roads have been roads.

---

My grandmother believed certain roads had histories that the roads themselves preserved. Not in any supernatural sense — she was not a woman who trafficked in the supernatural — but in a geological sense. A geological-plus-time sense. The land remembers pressure. It remembers what has passed over it. You drive a road long enough, she said, and you can tell whether it was a good road or a bad road — whether what used to travel it was traveling toward something or away.

She never specified what "bad" meant in this context. I've thought about it since. I think she meant: a road that something was fleeing. A road that runs away from instead of toward.

Twenty Mile Road, she told me once, without context, without anything before or after — *Twenty Mile Road, I wouldn't drive that one at night.*

She died before I thought to ask why.

---

The light this October has been particular. I've been out most evenings with a recorder, not a camera, gathering the sounds: fence wire in wind, the last insects before the cold, the specific acoustics of an empty road at dusk when traffic drops to almost nothing and what's left is the landscape settling.

I keep finding myself at the same pull-off, a mile south of where the road bends. There's a field there, harvested now, the stalks broken down to winter stubble. An old fence line running toward the tree margin. Something about the fence posts against that October light.

I don't photograph it. I just stand there.

I am being added to some account.
