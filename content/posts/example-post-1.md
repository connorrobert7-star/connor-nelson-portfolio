## The Threshold Problem

Every horror film has a threshold problem. Something must cross over from the unsafe zone into the zone we've designated as safe — as *ours* — for the horror to register. The house. The body. The family. The mind.

*Pulse* (2001) — Kiyoshi Kurosawa's internet horror film, the one all subsequent internet horror films have failed to learn from — proposes a different architecture. In *Pulse*, the threshold has already been crossed. It was crossed when we first went online. The safe zone has been compromised from the inside, and what we thought was us in the screen is something else wearing our shape.

This isn't a metaphor. Or rather, it is exactly the kind of metaphor that functions as literal description.

## The Forbidden Room

The film organizes itself around rooms. Sealed rooms. Rooms you find on the internet. Rooms inside computers. Rooms that contain something that was once human and is now something for which we don't have a word.

The image Kurosawa returns to is a figure in a darkened room, moving strangely, at the periphery of what a low-resolution feed can render. This was 2001 — before the resolution improved. The blurring, the artifact, the *not-quite-there-ness* of digital capture at that moment in its development is recruited for genuine dread. Something is visible because something is almost invisible.

---

What makes *Pulse* essential — still, twenty-three years later — is its understanding that the horror of connectivity is not what you might encounter but what you become. The ghosts in *Pulse* don't want to frighten you. They want you to be like them: present but not present, visible but unseeing, always available, always alone.

*I have seen enough of the internet to believe this is not inaccurate.*

The sequel films, the American remake, the countless films that have tried to mine this territory since — they've made the internet monstrous in legible ways. Virus. Predator. Stalker. Kurosawa understood something more patient: the internet is not predatory. It is simply, inexorably *there*. And its there-ness changes what you are.

## On Watching Now

To return to *Pulse* in the present decade is to find that Kurosawa was making a film about a future that has now arrived and been so thoroughly normalized that the dread is harder to locate. We live in the forbidden rooms. We have furnished them.

The question the film leaves, ultimately, is the one horror films are always leaving: *and then what do you do about it?*

In Kurosawa's film, you go somewhere with no signal. You find someone. You survive together, incompletely, on the edge of something.

That's the only answer that's ever available. Make of it what you will.
